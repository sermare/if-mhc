     Recovering and Redirecting Peptide Backbone Conformation via\\
     Contact-Conditioned C$\alpha$ Generation in a Cross-Reactive pMHC/TCR System\par}
    \vspace{5pt}
    {\normalsize Authors$^{*}$\par}
    \vspace{8pt}
  \end{center}

\noindent\textbf{Motivation.}
Some T-cell receptors recognize several different peptides by letting each peptide fold into a different
shape inside the same MHC groove [2]. Deep generative models can now build protein backbones with high
accuracy (RFdiffusion [1]), which raises a simple question: given only the receptor side of the
interaction, can such a model be steered to build a peptide backbone in one chosen target shape, even a
shape that is not the one closest to its input? We test this in a well-studied two-peptide system [2]: the
DMF5 receptor on HLA-A*02:01 binds two 10-residue peptides, GIG
(crystal 6AM5) and DRG (6AMU), whose backbones differ by 2.87\,\AA. That difference is almost entirely at
the tail end (the first four positions are nearly identical, within 1.14\,\AA, while position 6 moves
5.62\,\AA), and which shape, or register, a peptide takes is set by which residue drops into the MHC
F-pocket (position 10 for GIG, position 9 for DRG).

\noindent\textbf{Methods.}
Conditioning only on receptor-side contact residues, with no peptide-backbone template, we asked whether
RFdiffusion [1] can generate a C$\alpha$ backbone that recovers a peptide's own native register or crosses
into the alternate one. We generated 300 designs per crystal for each of 20 conditioning schemes, plus a
focused N-terminus-seeding experiment. Designs are scored against a three-criterion register test calibrated
not to a single crystal but to \textbf{native molecular-dynamics ensembles} (Amber ff19SB, TIP3P,
physiological \textbf{310\,K}, 50\,ns): C$\alpha$ proximity within a moving-block-bootstrap acceptance band
(DRG within 1.48\,\AA, 95\% CI 1.36 to 1.58), correct F-pocket occupant identity, and native burial depth.
We additionally correct for a forward/reverse backbone-threading artifact that raw RMSD cannot detect.

\noindent\textbf{Results.}
Both recovery and redirection are achievable but rare, and are gated by the breadth of conditioning rather
than by any specific curated residue set. Across the de novo corpus, 4 of 8{,}722 designs recover the native
DRG register (best 1.07\,\AA) and 3 of 8{,}722 cross into the alternate DRG register (best 1.44\,\AA), every
hit forward-threaded and seating the correct p9 anchor, all arising from the broadest conditioning schemes.
Critically, our no-conditioning control produced \textbf{zero} designs that dock in the groove or come close
in RMSD to either native backbone: \textbf{0 of 932} unconditioned draws entered the acceptance band or
seated an anchor, so the recovery and crossing events are real signal above a true zero-information
background, not sampling noise. A templating control that supplies backbone geometry directly recovers
register on demand, monotonically with the number of templated residues (best 0.10\,\AA), whereas contact
conditioning alone only weakly determines it, locating register in a spatially confined tail-end signal.
These results, from a single well-characterized system, map the current capability boundary of
contact-conditioned backbone generation and separate what it can retrieve from what must still be supplied
as explicit geometry.
